## Data Nugget

As promised, I went back from 2022 - 2024 to investigate the relationship between ESPN projections and player scores. I did not look at my personal pick rates this week so if anyone else wants to go back and check that be my guest.

This is also a fun adventure in data science. Our first plot looked a little funny;
there was an abundance of points with apparent zero projected points, yet many actual points. That struck me as wrong, so maybe I processed the data wrong. Once I ruled that out, I thought my scraping might not have worked. I clicked back to one of these instances and Voil√†! ESPN was literally listing their projections at zero. Not my fault. Look at your data kids. There's no apparent rhyme or reason for these missing projections, they're spread across weeks, years, players, and positions. Only 17 of the 252 matchups, so they were just excluded. Our first plot shows the correlation between ESPN projections and player scores.

<img src="{{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/21-24_proj_res_corr.png" alt="">

Our relative correlation values match expectations: D/ST has the lowest meaning its the least predictable, followed by TEs, then WRs, then RBs, and finally QBs. RBs and QBs' high correlation values may reflect their "high floor" outcomes, but is also intuitively correct as they each receive more than double digit opportunities. Variance in player score increases as opportunities per game decreases, leading to a greater departure on average from projections. Are the data any different if we just consider starters? 

Correlation values are much lower in this case since no backups / injured players are considered that are projected to score zero or just a point or two. However, the overall trends hold, yet with RB being slightly more predictable than QB. Notice those points with zero projected and scored points? Yea thats all yall missing surprise inactives for the most part. Of these 23 instances, 11 of them were Jeremy. No one else had more than 3. Removing those values further decreased our R-squared values but not by much.

<img src="{{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/21-24_proj_res_corr_starters.png" alt="">

Our next question is what percentage of the time the ESPN projected winner is the actual winner? The answer is simple enough to calculate, but lets visualize it!

<img src="{{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/21-24_proj_accuracy.png" alt="">

We've randomly assigned teams in a matchup as "team one" or "team two", so value differences being positive or negative simply reflects which team is favored. When both projected differences and actual differences are negative, the projected and actual result match (quadrants 1 and 3, colored green), yet when they are different signs (quadrants 2 and 4 colored red), the projected winner was not the actual winner.

ESPN projections correctly predict the winner nearly 63% of the time (n = 234). When a team is projected to win by 10 or more points, that value rises to over 68% (n = 116), and when it hits 20 or more points its up to 76% (n = 42).
